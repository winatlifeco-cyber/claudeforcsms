Chapter one · Your sidebar, top to bottom

Nine features, in order.

Nine items, top to bottom, in the order they appear in your app. What each one does, why it matters, and a CSM use case for every single one.

01
New chat
Start a fresh conversation with Claude

What it is

A blank chat. No memory of other conversations, no inherited context. A fresh slate every time you click it.

Think of it as opening a new Word document, not a continuation.

CSM use cases

  • One-off questions. "Rewrite this email to sound less corporate." Start a new chat, do it, move on.
  • Keep contexts separate. Don't mix a client escalation with a commissions question. Two different tasks, two different chats.
  • Public-sensitive work. Drafting a sensitive internal message? New chat, no prior context bleeding in.
Try this: Think of one throwaway question you asked Claude this week that was genuinely useful. That's the bar. Most "wins" with AI are small, repeated thousands of times.
02
Search
Find anything across every chat you've ever had

What it is

A search bar that looks across all your past chats. Type a keyword, get a list of every conversation that mentioned it.

Claude's memory doesn't work automatically across chats, but search lets you find the chat you need in seconds.

CSM use cases

  • Find past drafts. "What did I write to that client about their contract renewal last month?" Search the client name and open the chat.
  • Reuse winning language. You wrote a great pushback email three months ago. Search for the client name, find it, adapt it.
  • Audit your own work. Review past conversations before a difficult call to see what you've promised or said.
Search works on the content of chats, not just titles. Type a phrase you remember saying and Claude finds it.
03
Customize
Shape how Claude works with Skills and Connectors

What it is

The control center for Skills and Connectors. As the page itself puts it: "skills, connectors, and plugins shape how Claude works with you."

Connectors let Claude read and write to the tools you already use. Skills teach Claude your processes, team norms, and expertise. Both live here.

CSM use cases

  • Connect your apps. Gmail, Google Calendar, Drive, HubSpot, your data tools. Turn on what you use, turn off what you don't.
  • Teach Claude a process. Create a Skill for "Write a QBR summary in our house style." Once taught, Claude applies it every time you ask.
  • Codify team norms. A Skill like "Handle a churn risk" can encode the exact playbook every CSM should follow. Consistent quality, at scale.
Click Customize, then Create new skills. Teach Claude one thing you do weekly: a QBR format, a pushback structure, the way you handle a late payment.
04
Chats
Your full conversation history

What it is

Every chat you've ever had, listed. Rename the useful ones, delete the throwaways.

Reopening a chat gives Claude the full memory of that conversation. Pick up exactly where you left off, even weeks later.

CSM use cases

  • Name chats like you'd name emails. "Q2 escalation prep," "Renewal notes," "April QBR prep." Future-you will thank past-you.
  • Keep long-running threads alive. A chat about a specific account can run for weeks. Claude remembers everything in it.
  • Prune regularly. Delete old throwaway chats. Makes Search sharper.
Try this: Scroll through your chat history for a second. Find one chat that ended up being more useful than you expected. What made it work? That pattern is worth repeating.
05
Projects
Folders with persistent instructions and knowledge

What it is

A folder with two superpowers: project instructions (standing rules Claude follows in every chat) and project knowledge (documents Claude can reference).

Every chat you start inside a project inherits both. Build the context once, reuse it forever.

CSM use cases

  • A "My CSM" project. Instructions cover your tone and process. Knowledge holds the pushback playbook, the QBR template, the onboarding guide.
  • Per-account projects. One project per strategic account, with their contract terms, meeting notes, and recent tickets loaded. Start any chat inside it and Claude is instantly up to speed.
  • Shared team projects. One project every CSM uses. When you update it, everyone benefits.
In a project, write clear instructions: "Always use direct, accountable language. Never hedge. When drafting client emails, keep them under 150 words unless I say otherwise."
06
Enterprise knowledge
Your company's collective wisdom, in one question

What it is

On Enterprise plans, Claude can be wired into your company's internal knowledge. One question gets searched across Gmail, Drive, Microsoft 365, Asana, Notion, Confluence, and whatever else your team lives in.

Different platforms call it different things. The shape is always the same: you ask once, it searches everywhere, and the answer cites the source.

CSM use cases

  • Policy and process questions. "What's our standard position on backdating offboardings?" It searches Confluence, past Slack decisions, shared Drive docs, all in one query.
  • Historical context. "Has any team talked to this client's parent company before?" Instead of asking five people, ask once.
  • Cross-team answers. "What does Ops usually do when FX controls block a payment?" You get the answer without chasing down the right Slack channel.
Treat it as the first stop before you ask a human. If you were about to DM someone, ask the enterprise tool first. It often already knows.
07
Artifacts
Files and mini-apps Claude generates

What it is

When Claude creates something substantial (a document, a chart, a working app), it goes into an artifact: a dedicated panel next to the chat. The Artifacts tab in the sidebar collects all of yours in one place.

You can edit them in real time by chatting, publish them as links, and remix the ones other people share.

CSM use cases

  • Client-ready one-pagers. "Turn this data into a one-page client summary." Get a polished artifact, tweak the wording, send it.
  • Interactive dashboards. A renewal tracker, an FX calculator, a churn visualizer. Publish the link and share with your team.
  • Reusable templates. Build an artifact once (a QBR structure, a pricing proposal layout). Remix it for every client.
"Build me a simple web dashboard showing my top 10 accounts by MRR, with renewal dates and a risk flag." Claude generates the app. You iterate by chatting.
08
Code
Claude Code, the terminal-based coding agent

What it is

A shortcut into Claude Code, a version of Claude that runs in your terminal and edits files on your computer directly. Different from chat-based Claude: it has real access to your codebase.

For CSMs who don't write code: this is the "builders" door. You probably won't open it today. But it's how I've built every internal tool I use.

CSM use cases

  • Build internal tools. Churn trackers, QBR generators, commissions calculators. If you can describe it, Claude Code can build it.
  • Automate repetitive data work. "Read this folder of invoice CSVs and make me a monthly summary." Claude Code does it and saves it.
  • Prototype before asking engineering. If you need a new internal tool, build a working version first. The ask to Ops becomes way easier.
You don't need this today. Just know it exists, and that the path from "idea" to "working tool" is shorter than it's ever been.

Design NEW
Claude Design, visual design with a conversation

What it is

A separate Claude tool (at claude.ai/design) where you describe what you want to design and Claude produces it on a visual canvas. Currently in research preview.

It can be connected to a design system: your logo, your colors, your typography. Every output comes back on-brand.

CSM use cases

  • Client-ready visuals in minutes. "Design a one-page overview of our onboarding process." Get a branded layout, export to PDF, send it.
  • Renewal decks. Describe the story you want to tell. Claude Design lays it out. You iterate by chatting.
  • Internal process visuals. Org charts, escalation flows, stakeholder maps. Draft them visually instead of wrestling with PowerPoint.
"Design a 3-slide deck summarizing our Q1 client wins. Use a clean editorial feel. Include space for one headline stat per slide."

Chapter two · Beyond the sidebar

Two places Claude lives outside this window.

Claude isn't just what you see at claude.ai. Two other products belong in your toolkit. Both are built for the kind of work CSMs do every day, and both are underused by most teams.

Claude Cowork
Desktop agent · generally available

A desktop app where Claude doesn't just answer questions, it does the work. You give it a goal and access to your files, and it moves between your documents, folders, and apps to deliver a finished result.

Connects to Gmail, Google Drive, DocuSign, Zoom, and others. Has scheduled tasks, so you can say "every Friday, pull my metrics and draft the weekly report," and it just happens.

CSM use case "Scan my Downloads folder, find everything from this client, organize it by date, and write a one-paragraph summary of what happened this quarter." Cowork will actually do it. Claude chat will tell you how.
Claude in Chrome
Browser extension · beta

Claude in your browser sidebar, able to see the page you're on and take action. Summarize any article, fill forms, extract data from a table, triage email threads, navigate between tabs.

Available to all paid subscribers. Pro plans run on Haiku. Max plans get Sonnet and Opus too. Still in beta, so treat it as a tool for low-stakes tasks while it matures.

CSM use case Client asks for a competitor comparison. Claude in Chrome opens each competitor's site, reads their pricing, and drafts a side-by-side in 5 minutes. No copy-pasting.

A word on privacy and data

CSMs handle confidential client data every day. Fair question: is what I paste into Claude safe?

On Enterprise plans, conversations are not used to train models. Data stays within the contracted boundary. Connectors only access what you explicitly authorize, and the connection can be revoked anytime in Customize. On consumer plans, the defaults are different and worth reading before you paste anything sensitive.

The same rules that apply to Slack and email apply here: don't paste in things you wouldn't send to a colleague. For Chrome extension and Cowork specifically, start with low-stakes tasks on trusted sites before granting broader permissions.

Chapter three · The art of prompting

A better prompt beats a better model.

9 times out of 10, the difference between a useless answer and a ship-ready one is how you asked. This chapter: the framework, the five prompts worth memorizing, and a before-and-after that shows the difference.

The framework · five pieces of a great prompt

R
Role
Who is Claude in this moment? "You are a senior CSM." "You are a global payroll expert." "You are my copy editor."
C
Context
What does Claude need to know? The client, the situation, the constraint, the history. Paste it. Don't make Claude guess.
T
Task
What specifically do you want? "Draft an email." "Summarize this in 5 bullets." "Rewrite the second paragraph."
F
Format
What should the output look like? Word count, structure, tone, language. Specificity here saves you iteration cycles later.
E
Examples
Show, don't tell. One real example of the tone or format you want is worth a paragraph of description.

Not every prompt needs all five pieces. But when a prompt isn't working, it's almost always because one of them is missing.

See the difference · weak prompt vs strong prompt

Weak prompt
"Write an email to my client about the invoice issue."
Strong prompt
You are my CSM assistant. Draft an apology email to the billing contact at one of my client accounts. Context: we double-charged their April invoice by $4,200. We've already refunded it. They noticed before we did. Tone: direct, accountable, no excuses. 100 words max. Sign off as Juan. Do not use em dashes. Example opening line I like: "I owe you a direct explanation."

The strong prompt has a role, context, task, format, and an example. It also tells Claude what not to do. You'll get a usable email on the first try.

Five prompts every CSM should have in their back pocket

01 · The escalation triage
When an angry email lands
You are my CSM assistant. I just received this email from a client. Analyze it for me: 1. What is the actual ask, stripped of emotion? 2. What is the emotional temperature (1-10)? 3. What are three possible root causes? 4. What internal teams do I probably need to loop in? 5. Draft a 3-sentence holding reply that buys me 24 hours without committing to anything. Email: [paste]
Turns a 20-minute panic into a 30-second plan.
02 · The QBR prep
Before every client review
You are preparing me for a QBR with [client]. Using the documents in this Project plus anything you can pull from my connected tools: 1. Three biggest wins this quarter, with numbers. 2. Two risks I need to surface, with my recommendation for each. 3. One expansion opportunity worth pitching. 4. The three questions the client is most likely to ask me. 5. A one-sentence opening for the call that sets the right tone. Output as a one-page brief I can read on my phone while I walk to the meeting.
Use inside a Project that has the client's history loaded.
03 · The pushback
When a client asks for too much
You are my CSM copywriter. A client has asked us to [do the thing]. Our position is that we can't, because [reason]. But we can offer [alternative]. Draft a reply that: - Acknowledges their ask without repeating it back - States our position firmly but warmly - Offers the alternative as a real solution, not a consolation prize - Ends with a clear next step - Under 120 words - No hedging. No "I totally understand." No em dashes.
The "no hedging" line is what turns a good draft into a great one.
04 · The meeting debrief
After every client call
Here are my raw notes from a client call. Turn them into: 1. A 4-bullet summary for my manager. 2. A list of action items, each with owner and deadline. 3. The follow-up email to the client, in my voice, under 150 words. 4. Three questions I should think about before the next call. Notes: [paste]
Run this while the call is still fresh. Saves 30 minutes of "how did I word that action item again?"
05 · The honest review
Before you send anything
Review this draft email as if you were the client receiving it. Be honest, not polite. Specifically: 1. Where will they push back? 2. What sounds like corporate hedging? 3. What would a skeptical reader call out? 4. What's the weakest sentence? 5. Rewrite the weakest sentence in a stronger way. Draft: [paste]
The closest thing Claude can do to a second pair of eyes. Use it before anything sensitive.

Claude Code builds. GitHub remembers. Vercel hosts. Supabase stores. That's the whole pipeline.
Claude Code
Where you work

A version of Claude that runs as a command-line tool on your laptop. Instead of copying code between a chat window and your editor, Claude reads and edits your files directly, runs commands, checks its own work, and fixes its own bugs.

You talk to it in plain English. "Build me a dashboard that pulls from this CSV." "Add a filter to the renewals page." "Fix the bug where the date is wrong." It does the work, shows you the diff, asks before doing anything destructive.

The real shift: you stop thinking in code and start thinking in requirements. You become the product manager. Claude Code is the engineer.

The tool that turned "I'd need an engineer" into "I can do this myself."
GitHub
Where code lives

A storage system for code, built for teams. Every version of every file is saved forever. You can see what changed, who changed it, and roll back if something breaks.

When Claude Code finishes a change, it commits that change to GitHub. You also get free project management: Issues for tracking bugs, Pull Requests for reviewing changes, Actions for automating things like tests.

Bonus: public GitHub profiles are becoming the new LinkedIn for builders. Shipping something good in public is a credential.

Google Docs' version history, but for code, and far more powerful.
Vercel
Where the app runs

A hosting service that takes your GitHub code and turns it into a live, visitable website. Connect your repo once. Every push rebuilds and deploys the app in under a minute.

Every deployment gets its own unique URL, so you can share a work-in-progress with someone before it's "real." Preview links are a superpower when you want feedback without committing.

Alternatives you'll also hear: Netlify, Railway, Render, Cloudflare Pages. Same idea, different flavors.

If GitHub is where the recipe is written, Vercel is the kitchen where it cooks.
Supabase
Where the data lives

A database as a service. Your app needs to remember things between visits: users, records, settings, history. That data lives in Supabase.

What you actually get: a real Postgres database (the same one banks use), plus user authentication, file storage, and real-time updates, all wrapped in a clean interface. You didn't need a backend engineer. Supabase is the backend.

Alternatives: Firebase (Google), Neon, PlanetScale. Supabase is the most popular right now because it plays nicely with the Claude Code workflow.

The filing cabinet behind the app. Nothing useful works without it.

Terminal

A text-based window where you type commands instead of clicking buttons. Every computer has one. On a Mac it's called Terminal, on Windows it's PowerShell or Command Prompt.

It looks intimidating (black background, blinking cursor), but it's just a different way to talk to your computer. Click a folder with your mouse, or type cd folder-name in the terminal. Same thing, different interface.

You open Terminal. You type: claude. Claude Code starts a session right there in the window.

Chapter seven · A few cautions

Where Claude doesn't belong.

A short list of moments where the right answer is still a human one. Save yourself the lesson of learning these the hard way.

Before you get too excited

Some things Claude should never do for you.

  • Compensation or commission decisions without a human reviewing the output. Claude can do the math; you own the judgment.
  • Client-facing messages sent without being read first. Draft with Claude, send as yourself. Never auto-send.
  • HR-sensitive matters as the primary tool. Terminations, disputes, grievances go to People Ops, not an AI draft.
  • Replace direct conversations with important stakeholders. If a client deserves a call, give them the call. Don't hide behind a polished email.
  • Store credentials, API keys, or PII in chat history. It's not a vault. Treat conversations like shared notes.
  • Legal, tax, or compliance sign-off on anything regulated. Claude drafts. Legal approves. That order, always.
